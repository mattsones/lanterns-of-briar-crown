# Liam’s Game — Chapter 1 Story Script

This document pulls the story-facing text out of the current prototype so the chapter can be reviewed like a script, without needing to play through every branch.

It is organized by location, scene, and state. Button labels are included where they affect story flow or player tone.

## Opening / Character Start

## South Gate / Bramble Boar

**Story purpose of this revision**

The boar encounter should not feel like a random tutorial fight with a clue attached afterward. The player should understand three things at once:

1. The village is in immediate danger.
2. The courier is a real person, not just a missing plot object.
3. The satchel creates the bridge from “wild animal problem” to “organized deception.”

The satchel should contain both an official clue and a human detail.

### First boar encounter
**Text**

The south gate bursts with shouting.

A Bramble Boar crashes out of the brush, wild-eyed and foaming, a courier satchel twisted around one tusk. It is not attacking like a hungry beast. It is running like something has driven it mad.

The satchel thumps against its jaw with every charge. A brass courier badge flashes once in the dust, bright enough for someone nearby to gasp.

“That's Lio Brindle's route badge,” Nella whispers from behind a shutter. “He was due before breakfast.”

The boar lowers its head toward the square. If it breaks through, people will be hurt—and whatever happened to Lio will vanish down the road behind it.

**Choices**
- Stand and fight before it reaches the square.
- Try to draw it away from town.

---

### If player tries to draw it away
**Text**

You feint toward the road, waving and shouting, trying to pull the boar’s attention away from the square.

For a heartbeat, it works. The boar turns. The courier satchel swings wide, and you see a cut strap whipping loose beside the brass badge.

Then the animal scrapes the dirt and lunges back toward the gate. There is no clean escape without leaving the village exposed. This fight is coming whether you like it or not.

**Choices**
- Then I stand and fight.
- Back away for now.

### Battle reward after victory
**Title**

Courier Satchel

**Text**

The boar collapses in a storm of dust and snapping brambles. When it finally lies still, the square does not cheer right away. Everyone is looking at the satchel.

The leather is scuffed, but the courier badge is still readable: **Lio Brindle, South Lantern Route**.

Inside, you find a folded order bearing a crown seal that looks right until you stare at it too long. The crown points are uneven. The wax smells faintly of pine pitch instead of royal resin.

The order reads:

**DELAY ROADS. STIR PANIC. HOLD ALL MESSENGERS.**

Beneath it is something worse than the order: a small lunch packet, still tied with blue string. Someone has tucked a note under the knot in a careful hand:

**Lio — back before noon, or I’m eating your pear roll. — Mara**

Whoever carried this satchel expected to reach Hearthhollow today. Whoever cut it loose did not want him arriving at all.

**Choice**
- Take the order and the badge to follow the trail.

## Lantern Shrine

**Story purpose of this revision**

The Lantern Shrine should become one of Chapter 1’s symbolic anchors, not just a stat blessing. It teaches the player the chapter’s central contrast:

- **True road signs** guide, remember, and protect travelers.
- **False authority** commands, misdirects, and uses fear.

The shrine introduces the phrase:

**A road is safest when truth walks it first.**

That phrase can echo later through Enna’s investigation and the sealed-door ending, tying the title **Lanterns of Briar Crown** more tightly to the chapter’s themes.

---

### First visit
**Text**

A waist-high shrine stands where Lantern Road dips between two old stones, half-hidden by violet moss and old candle-stubs. The bronze lantern inside is unlit, but the glass catches sunlight that is not falling from the sky.

Scratched names cover the shrine: drivers, couriers, pilgrims, market children practicing letters, and little route marks carved by people who needed the road to remember them kindly. Some marks are plain arrows. Some are lanterns. Some are only initials, pressed deep by worried hands before long journeys.

Around the base, worn almost smooth by weather and touch, is an old traveler saying:

**A road is safest when truth walks it first.**

But one fresh mark cuts across the older names: a false crown seal, copied badly. It has been scratched over the lantern marks as if command could erase memory.

**Choices**
- Clean the false seal from the shrine.
- Study the true road marks.
- Leave the shrine untouched.

---

### If player studies the true road marks
**Text**

Most of the shrine marks are old, but not random. Couriers used small lantern scratches to show safe turnoffs. Traders carved leaf marks near supply caches. Roadwardens left little shield cuts beside dangerous bends.

These signs do not order anyone around. They do not pretend to be royal. They simply tell the truth to the next traveler:

**Water here. Shelter north. Broken bridge east. Safe camp beyond the pines.**

Then you notice three recent courier marks scratched in a hurry, each beside a tiny arrow pointing toward Bramblecross. Someone used this shrine as a quiet route marker before the false crown was carved over it.

The shrine has not been silent. It has been interrupted.

**Choices**
- Clean the false seal and honor the true marks.
- Leave the shrine for now.

---

### If player cleans the false seal
**Text**

You scrape away the false crown mark carefully, following the shallow cuts until the copied seal breaks apart into meaningless scratches. Under it, the older lantern lines are still there.

For a moment, the shrine’s bronze lantern glows—not brightly, not dramatically, but warmly, like a small truth refusing to be embarrassed by darkness.

The old saying seems easier to read now:

**A road is safest when truth walks it first.**

The warmth moves through your hand and steadies your breath. Whatever is happening on Lantern Road, not every old sign has been corrupted. Some lights still remember what they were made for.

**Outcome text**

The shrine warms under your hand. HP +8 • Will +1

**Story implication**

The player has restored a true lantern mark and learned how to distinguish helpful road signs from false authority. This makes later route clues feel earned rather than arbitrary.

---

The false crown mark is gone. Beneath it, the true road signs remain: shelter, water, warning, witness.

Around the base, the old traveler saying holds steady:

**A road is safest when truth walks it first.**

**Choice**

She does not need to.

**Choices**
- I'll follow Westroot.
- If Lio is alive, I'll find him.
- The Briar Crown won't get to bury this.

**Text**

That night, Bramblecross does not sleep easily.

But it sleeps with one more truth than it had before.

The road was not simply dangerous. It was being lied about.

The cellar was not merely haunted. It was part of an older way.

### Companion reactions at key Chapter 1 beats

Companion reactions should be sparse but meaningful. They do not need to comment on every clue. They should speak when the story meaning changes.

---

#### After Enna completes the case wall

**Rowan**

Rowan studies the pins and strings without touching them.

"That is not bandit work," he says. "Bandits take what is loose. This is someone deciding where fear should stand. I do not like fighting plans, but I like leaving them alone even less."

**Tilda**

Tilda leans close to the board, then whistles softly.

"Oh, that is ugly. Clever ugly, but ugly. Fake orders here, missing cargo there, frightened travelers in between. Someone made a maze and convinced people to call it a road."

**Moss**

Moss looks from the forged orders to the older route marks.

"A road remembers how it was treated," they say. "This one has been taught to flinch. That can be untaught, but not by force alone."

---

#### After discovering the Briar Crown mark

**Rowan**

Rowan tightens his grip on his shield.

"That mark wants obedience before trust. I know the shape of that. Whoever uses it expects people to kneel first and ask questions later."

**Tilda**

Tilda crouches beside the mark and wrinkles her nose.

"Not royal. Not honest. Not even stylish. If you are going to impersonate authority, at least have the decency to draw straight lines."

Then her grin fades.

"Still. People believed it from a distance. That is what worries me."

**Moss**

Moss does not touch the mark.

"Briars protect living things when they grow where they belong," they say. "This was carved to choke something older. That is not growth. That is conquest pretending to be nature."

---

#### At the sealed iron door

**Rowan**

Rowan lowers his voice.

"Edden reached this far and still left proof. That is courage. Quiet courage counts."

**Tilda**

Tilda turns the phrase over in her mouth: "Willow seal recovered. Courier unaccounted for."

Her grin is gone.

"That is not a report. That is inventory. I hate people who inventory people."

**Moss**

Moss rests a hand near the sealed door, not touching it.

"There is still lantern-light under the roots," they say. "Buried, but not gone. That matters."

---

#### During the report-back scene

**Rowan**

"They are using roads like weapons," Rowan says. "Then we answer by making roads safe again. One route at a time."

**Tilda**

"Westroot opened," Tilda repeats. "That sounds like a door someone forgot to tell us was a door. I love finding those. I hate what they probably moved through it."

**Moss**

Moss watches the gold lanternlight reflected in the cracked glass on Hollis's desk.

"Truth walked first once," they say. "It can again. But roads remember every footstep. We should choose ours carefully."

# Useful Current Story Questions

These are the biggest story-review questions suggested by the script:

1. Does Hearthhollow’s urgency feel strong enough before the player leaves?
2. Does the satchel/boar/courier mystery clearly motivate the player onto Lantern Road?
3. Does Lantern Road escalate from “wild animal problem” to “organized deception” smoothly?
4. Does the traveler add useful color without becoming a required quest bottleneck?
5. Does Ada’s cart quest feel naturally connected to the main investigation without being mandatory?
6. Does Enna feel like she is constructing the case from your information rather than already knowing everything?
7. Does Hollis’s gatekeeping make sense, or does it feel like arbitrary RPG gating?
8. Does the Root Cellar reveal enough about the wider Chapter 2 mystery without overexplaining?
9. Do Rowan, Tilda, and Moss need richer recruitment conversations before art pass?
10. Does the title “Lanterns of Briar Crown” feel supported by the shrine/road/cellar imagery?
